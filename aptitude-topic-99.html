Mastering Percentages & Interest Calculations

Comprehensive guide to understanding and solving problems on Percentages, Simple Interest, and Compound Interest for competitive exams.

Percentages Simple Interest Compound Interest

Introduction

Percentages, Simple Interest, and Compound Interest are fundamental concepts in quantitative aptitude that appear frequently in various competitive exams including banking, SSC, railways, and other government recruitment tests. These topics are not only important for exams but also have practical applications in everyday financial decisions.

In this comprehensive guide, we'll cover each concept in detail with formulas, step-by-step examples, shortcuts, and practice problems to help you master these topics.

Percentages

Percentage is a number or ratio expressed as a fraction of 100. It is denoted by the symbol "%". The word percentage comes from the Latin word 'per centum' which means 'by the hundred'.

Percentage = (Value / Total Value) × 100%

Value = (Percentage × Total Value) / 100%

Total Value = (Value × 100%) / Percentage

Where:

  • Value is the quantity you want to express as a percentage
  • Total Value is the whole amount
  • Percentage is the ratio expressed per hundred

Example 1: Finding a Percentage

Question: What is 25% of 480?

Identify the percentage (25%) and the total value (480).
Use the formula: Value = (Percentage × Total Value) / 100%
Value = (25 × 480) / 100 = 12,000 / 100 = 120
Answer: 25% of 480 is 120.

Example 2: Finding What Percentage One Number is of Another

Question: 36 is what percentage of 144?

Identify the value (36) and the total value (144).
Use the formula: Percentage = (Value / Total Value) × 100%
Percentage = (36 / 144) × 100% = 0.25 × 100% = 25%
Answer: 36 is 25% of 144.

Example 3: Finding the Total Value

Question: 42 is 35% of what number?

Identify the value (42) and the percentage (35%).
Use the formula: Total Value = (Value × 100%) / Percentage
Total Value = (42 × 100%) / 35% = 4,200 / 35 = 120
Answer: 42 is 35% of 120.

Percentage Shortcuts

  • Finding 10%: Simply move the decimal point one place to the left. For example, 10% of 250 = 25.
  • Finding 5%: Find 10% and then divide by 2. For example, 5% of 250 = 10% of 250 ÷ 2 = 25 ÷ 2 = 12.5.
  • Finding 20%: Find 10% and then multiply by 2. For example, 20% of 250 = 10% of 250 × 2 = 25 × 2 = 50.
  • Finding 25%: Divide by 4. For example, 25% of 250 = 250 ÷ 4 = 62.5.
  • Finding 50%: Divide by 2. For example, 50% of 250 = 250 ÷ 2 = 125.
  • Finding 75%: Find 50% and add 25%. For example, 75% of 250 = 125 + 62.5 = 187.5.

Real-World Applications

  • Discounts and Sales: "20% off" means you pay 80% of the original price.
  • Tax Calculations: Income tax, sales tax, and GST are all calculated using percentages.
  • Academic Grading: Exam scores are often expressed as percentages.
  • Business: Profit and loss are calculated as percentages of cost price.
  • Statistics: Data analysis often involves percentage calculations.

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

If the price of a shirt is marked as ₹800 after a 20% increase, what was its original price?

Let the original price be x.

After 20% increase, the new price = x + 20% of x = x + 0.2x = 1.2x

Given that 1.2x = ₹800

Therefore, x = ₹800 / 1.2 = ₹666.67

Answer: The original price was ₹666.67

Problem 2:

In an examination, 70% of the students passed in Mathematics and 80% passed in Science. If 10% of the students failed in both subjects, what percentage of students passed in both subjects?

Let's denote:

- Students who passed in Mathematics: 70%

- Students who passed in Science: 80%

- Students who failed in both: 10%

Students who failed in Mathematics = 100% - 70% = 30%

Students who failed in Science = 100% - 80% = 20%

Using the formula: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

Where P(A ∪ B) is the percentage of students who passed in at least one subject.

We know that students who failed in both = 10%, so students who passed in at least one subject = 100% - 10% = 90%

90% = 70% + 80% - P(both)

P(both) = 70% + 80% - 90% = 60%

Answer: 60% of students passed in both subjects.

Problem 3:

A shopkeeper marks his goods 25% above the cost price and gives a discount of 10% on the marked price. What is his profit percentage?

Let's say the cost price is ₹100.

Marked price = ₹100 + 25% of ₹100 = ₹100 + ₹25 = ₹125

Discount = 10% of ₹125 = ₹12.5

Selling price = ₹125 - ₹12.5 = ₹112.5

Profit = ₹112.5 - ₹100 = ₹12.5

Profit percentage = (₹12.5 / ₹100) × 100% = 12.5%

Answer: The profit percentage is 12.5%.

Simple Interest

Simple Interest is the interest calculated only on the initial principal amount. It does not take into account the accumulated interest of previous periods.

SI = (P × R × T) / 100

A = P + SI = P + (P × R × T) / 100 = P(1 + RT/100)

Where:

  • SI = Simple Interest
  • P = Principal (initial amount)
  • R = Rate of interest per annum (%)
  • T = Time period (in years)
  • A = Amount (Principal + Interest)

Example 1: Calculating Simple Interest

Question: Calculate the simple interest on ₹5,000 at 6% per annum for 2 years.

Identify the principal (P = ₹5,000), rate (R = 6%), and time (T = 2 years).
Use the formula: SI = (P × R × T) / 100
SI = (5,000 × 6 × 2) / 100 = 60,000 / 100 = ₹600
Answer: The simple interest is ₹600.

Example 2: Finding the Principal

Question: The simple interest on a sum of money for 3 years at 8% per annum is ₹1,200. Find the principal amount.

Identify the simple interest (SI = ₹1,200), rate (R = 8%), and time (T = 3 years).
Use the formula: SI = (P × R × T) / 100 and solve for P.
1,200 = (P × 8 × 3) / 100
1,200 = 24P / 100
P = (1,200 × 100) / 24 = 120,000 / 24 = ₹5,000
Answer: The principal amount is ₹5,000.

Example 3: Finding the Rate of Interest

Question: A sum of ₹8,000 amounts to ₹9,600 in 4 years at simple interest. Find the rate of interest per annum.

Identify the principal (P = ₹8,000), amount (A = ₹9,600), and time (T = 4 years).
Calculate the simple interest: SI = A - P = ₹9,600 - ₹8,000 = ₹1,600
Use the formula: SI = (P × R × T) / 100 and solve for R.
1,600 = (8,000 × R × 4) / 100
1,600 = 32,000R / 100
R = (1,600 × 100) / 32,000 = 160,000 / 32,000 = 5%
Answer: The rate of interest is 5% per annum.

Simple Interest Shortcuts

  • When R × T = 100: SI = P (This means the interest equals the principal)
  • When P = 100: SI = R × T (The interest equals the product of rate and time)
  • For monthly calculations: SI = (P × R × T) / (100 × 12) where T is in months
  • For daily calculations: SI = (P × R × T) / (100 × 365) where T is in days

Real-World Applications

  • Fixed Deposits: Banks often use simple interest for short-term deposits.
  • Loans: Some personal loans and short-term loans use simple interest.
  • Treasury Bills: Government securities often use simple interest.
  • Savings Accounts: Some savings accounts calculate interest using simple interest.

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

A sum of money becomes ₹13,000 after 3 years and ₹15,000 after 5 years at simple interest. Find the sum and the rate of interest.

Let the principal be P and the rate of interest be R%.

After 3 years: P + (P × R × 3) / 100 = 13,000

After 5 years: P + (P × R × 5) / 100 = 15,000

From the first equation: P(1 + 3R/100) = 13,000 ... (1)

From the second equation: P(1 + 5R/100) = 15,000 ... (2)

Dividing equation (2) by equation (1):

(1 + 5R/100) / (1 + 3R/100) = 15,000 / 13,000 = 15/13

13(1 + 5R/100) = 15(1 + 3R/100)

13 + 13(5R/100) = 15 + 15(3R/100)

13 + 65R/100 = 15 + 45R/100

65R/100 - 45R/100 = 15 - 13

20R/100 = 2

R = 10%

Substituting R = 10% in equation (1):

P(1 + 3 × 10/100) = 13,000

P(1 + 0.3) = 13,000

1.3P = 13,000

P = 13,000 / 1.3 = 10,000

Answer: The principal is ₹10,000 and the rate of interest is 10% per annum.

Problem 2:

A sum of money lent out at simple interest amounts to ₹720 after 2 years and to ₹1,020 after 5 years. Find the sum and the rate of interest.

Let the principal be P and the rate of interest be R%.

After 2 years: P + (P × R × 2) / 100 = 720

After 5 years: P + (P × R × 5) / 100 = 1,020

From the first equation: P(1 + 2R/100) = 720 ... (1)

From the second equation: P(1 + 5R/100) = 1,020 ... (2)

Subtracting equation (1) from equation (2):

P(1 + 5R/100) - P(1 + 2R/100) = 1,020 - 720

P(5R/100 - 2R/100) = 300

P(3R/100) = 300

3PR/100 = 300

PR = 10,000

Substituting in equation (1):

P + 2PR/100 = 720

P + 2(10,000)/100 = 720

P + 200 = 720

P = 520

Since PR = 10,000, R = 10,000/520 = 19.23%

Answer: The principal is ₹520 and the rate of interest is 19.23% per annum.

Compound Interest

Compound Interest is the interest calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest of previous periods. It's essentially "interest on interest."

A = P(1 + R/100)^T

CI = A - P = P[(1 + R/100)^T - 1]

Where:

  • A = Final amount (Principal + Interest)
  • P = Principal (initial amount)
  • R = Rate of interest per annum (%)
  • T = Time period (in years)
  • CI = Compound Interest

Example 1: Calculating Compound Interest

Question: Calculate the compound interest on ₹5,000 at 10% per annum for 2 years, compounded annually.

Identify the principal (P = ₹5,000), rate (R = 10%), and time (T = 2 years).
Use the formula: A = P(1 + R/100)^T
A = 5,000(1 + 10/100)^2 = 5,000(1.1)^2 = 5,000 × 1.21 = ₹6,050
CI = A - P = ₹6,050 - ₹5,000 = ₹1,050
Answer: The compound interest is ₹1,050.

Example 2: Compound Interest with Different Compounding Periods

Question: Calculate the compound interest on ₹10,000 at 8% per annum for 2 years, compounded quarterly.

Identify the principal (P = ₹10,000), rate (R = 8%), and time (T = 2 years).
For quarterly compounding, we need to adjust the formula: A = P(1 + R/(100 × 4))^(4T)
A = 10,000(1 + 8/(100 × 4))^(4 × 2) = 10,000(1 + 0.02)^8 = 10,000(1.02)^8
A = 10,000 × 1.1717 = ₹11,717
CI = A - P = ₹11,717 - ₹10,000 = ₹1,717
Answer: The compound interest is ₹1,717.

Compound Interest Shortcuts

  • For 2 years at R%: CI ≈ SI + (SI × R) / 100
  • For doubling money: Rule of 72 - Time to double ≈ 72 / R
  • For small rates and short periods: CI ≈ SI + (SI × R × T(T-1)) / (2 × 100)
  • For half-yearly compounding: Use R/2 as rate and 2T as time in the formula
  • For quarterly compounding: Use R/4 as rate and 4T as time in the formula

Real-World Applications

  • Bank Deposits: Most banks use compound interest for fixed deposits and recurring deposits.
  • Loans: Home loans, car loans, and credit card interest are typically calculated using compound interest.
  • Investments: Mutual funds, stocks, and other investments grow through compound interest.
  • Retirement Planning: Compound interest plays a crucial role in retirement planning and pension funds.

Simple Interest vs. Compound Interest

Feature Simple Interest Compound Interest
Interest Calculation Only on principal On principal and accumulated interest
Formula SI = (P × R × T) / 100 CI = P[(1 + R/100)^T - 1]
Growth Linear growth Exponential growth
Amount after T years A = P(1 + RT/100) A = P(1 + R/100)^T
Typical Applications Short-term loans, treasury bills Bank deposits, home loans, investments
Benefit to Investor Lower returns Higher returns over long periods
Benefit to Borrower Lower cost of borrowing Higher cost of borrowing

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

A sum of money invested at compound interest amounts to ₹16,000 after 2 years and to ₹17,280 after 3 years. Find the rate of interest and the sum.

Let the principal be P and the rate of interest be R%.

After 2 years: P(1 + R/100)^2 = 16,000 ... (1)

After 3 years: P(1 + R/100)^3 = 17,280 ... (2)

Dividing equation (2) by equation (1):

(1 + R/100) = 17,280 / 16,000 = 1.08

1 + R/100 = 1.08

R/100 = 0.08

R = 8%

Substituting R = 8% in equation (1):

P(1 + 8/100)^2 = 16,000

P(1.08)^2 = 16,000

P × 1.1664 = 16,000

P = 16,000 / 1.1664 = 13,717.42

Answer: The principal is approximately ₹13,717 and the rate of interest is 8% per annum.

Problem 2:

The difference between compound interest and simple interest on a certain sum for 2 years at 5% per annum is ₹25. Find the sum.

Let the principal be P.

Simple Interest for 2 years at 5%: SI = (P × 5 × 2) / 100 = P × 0.1

Amount after 2 years with Simple Interest: P + SI = P + P × 0.1 = 1.1P

Amount after 2 years with Compound Interest: P(1 + 5/100)^2 = P(1.05)^2 = P × 1.1025

Compound Interest: CI = P × 1.1025 - P = 0.1025P

Given that CI - SI = 25:

0.1025P - 0.1P = 25

0.0025P = 25

P = 25 / 0.0025 = 10,000

Answer: The sum is ₹10,000.
